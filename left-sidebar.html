Dolore Amet Consequat

In this project, I attempted to predict heart disease or attack cases based on a variety of potentially correlated health factors. The data to be analyzed comes from the Heart Disease Health Indicators Dataset sourced from Kaggle (https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/alexteboul/heart-disease-health-indicators-dataset), which is itself a subset of the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) dataset collected in 2015 by the CDC (https://www.cdc.gov/brfss/annual_data/2015/pdf/codebook15_llcp.pdf). Features in the dataset include relevant physical factors such as smoker status, diabetes diagnosis, and body mass index (BMI).

Adding to the complexity of this problem, the data is very imbalanced toward non-heart disease/heart attack cases (about 10:1). This means that overly simplistic models will not consistently predict disease cases, and accuracy alone will not be a proper metric of success (since greater than 90% accuracy can be achieved by simply predicting all cases as non-disease) . Instead we must look primarily to f1 score, a blend of precision and recall, which will better alert us to an unwanted bias toward false negative predictions. In addition to such interesting technical aspects, this study is highly pertinent given the relevance of this disease to our society today. Heart disease is consistently one of the leading causes of death in America. It goes without saying that the ability to predict it before it occurs will go a long way in saving lives.
